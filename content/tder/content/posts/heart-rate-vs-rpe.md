# Is Your Heart Rate Lying to You? The Case for RPE Over Zones in Fatigued States

You check your watch. The number is low. The legs feel like lead. You panic. You think you are lazy, or worse, you are under-recovered. You force the pace, chasing the number, burning glycogen you don't have.

This is the trap of the hobbyist. The trap of relying solely on heart rate (HR) as a proxy for effort.

## The Physiology of the Flatline

The data provided suggests a correlation between perceived exertion and a specific heart rate range (120–136 bpm, Zone 70) during late-afternoon runs. This is the baseline for a fresh, rested system. But physiology is not a static equation; it is a dynamic response to stress.

When accumulated fatigue sets in-whether from volume, intensity, or life stress-the autonomic nervous system (ANS) shifts. The parasympathetic system (rest and digest) ramps up to protect the heart, while the sympathetic system (fight or flight) is dampened.

This results in a phenomenon known as **cardiac drift** in reverse. Your heart rate drops, but your running economy degrades. The legs are heavy because of neuromuscular inhibition, not because your cardiovascular engine is lazy.

## The Contrarian View: Chase the Feeling, Not the Number

Most training plans dictate: "Run at Zone 2." They treat the body as a machine with a fixed input-output ratio. This is a fallacy.

Elite runners and ambitious athletes abandon strict HR zones during easy runs specifically because of accumulated fatigue. Here is why the "strict zone" approach is flawed in a fatigued state:

1.  **HR is a Lagging Indicator:** HR responds to fatigue after the fact. By the time your heart rate has elevated to the target zone, you have already expended too much energy.
2.  **The Central Governor Theory:** Tim Noakes’ theory suggests the brain limits effort to prevent damage. If your legs are heavy, the brain is telling you to slow down. Ignoring that signal and forcing the HR up is a recipe for injury and burnout.
3.  **The Zone 70 Trap:** The input data mentions a Zone 70 correlation. If you are fatigued, running at Zone 70 feels like a sprint. You are effectively running anaerobic threshold work while thinking you are doing an easy recovery run.

## The Practical Application: RPE is King

We do not abandon data; we interpret it differently. We use **Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)** as the primary dial for easy running, with HR serving only as a secondary check.

If your legs feel heavy, and you check your watch to see your HR is 10 beats lower than usual, **do not force the HR up.**

*   **The Signal:** Low HR + Heavy Legs = High Neuromuscular Fatigue.
*   **The Action:** Slow down. The pace doesn't matter as much as the effort. A 9:00/mile with a heavy leg feel is more valuable than an 8:30/mile with a light leg feel if the HR is artificially suppressed.

## The Takeaway

Stop chasing the chest strap. The strap measures the output of the heart, not the input of the muscles. When fatigue accumulates, the heart is trying to save you; the legs are trying to fail you. Trust the legs.

*   **Do this tomorrow:** Run an easy session based on RPE (Zone 2 effort) rather than HR. If your HR is significantly lower than your training zones, accept it as a sign of fatigue and run slower. Do not force the number.
